Training Plan Overview

Gilbert Days Half Marathon — November 21, 2026 in Arizona.

Goal: Run the entire 13.1 miles at 10:00/mile pace. Finish time: ~2:11.

This is your first half marathon. The plan builds you up gradually over 21 weeks so you arrive at the start line confident and healthy.

Phase 1 (Weeks 1-6): Incline treadmill only. Build from 27 to 37 min runs at 8% grade. Fix IT band with daily hip strengthening. Test flat running at week 4-5.

Phase 2 (Weeks 7-14): Transition to flat/outdoor running. Easy runs grow from 3.75 to 5.5 miles. Long runs build from 5.75 to 11 miles. Introduce tempo (race pace) runs.

Phase 3 (Weeks 15-20): Peak mileage. Long runs hit 13 miles then taper. Practice race pace. Final 2 weeks are recovery to arrive fresh.

Race Week: Short shakeout runs Mon/Wed. Rest Thu/Fri. Race Saturday.

Why incline first? The 8% grade reduces the IT band stress while giving a harder cardio workout. You're burning 50% more calories than flat at the same speed. Once the hip muscles are strong enough (weeks 4-6), flat running should be pain-free.

The problem: Your IT band isn't the issue itself — it's getting overloaded because your hip abductors (glute medius) aren't stabilizing your pelvis properly during running.

Why incline helps: Running uphill reduces the eccentric loading on the IT band (your leg doesn't fully straighten on the downhill phase). That's why you have zero pain at 8% incline.

The fix: Daily hip strengthening (clamshells, glute bridges, leg raises) builds the stabilizers so they can handle flat/outdoor running again. The stretches keep the TFL and surrounding tissue mobile.

Timeline: Most runners resolve IT band syndrome in 4-6 weeks of consistent hip work. You'll test flat running at week 4-5. If pain-free, you're cleared to transition.

Key indicator: When you can run 4+ miles on flat ground without lateral knee pain, you're past it.

Why: Strength supports injury prevention, running economy, and your push-up/pull-up goals. Stronger legs = more resilient joints for 13.1 miles.

Upper body (Tuesday): Push-ups build toward floor level. Rows and press build balanced upper body. Dead hangs and negative pull-ups build toward your first pull-up (expect 6-8 weeks). Band pull-aparts fix desk posture and neck pain.

Lower body (Thursday): Squats, RDLs, and lunges build the quad/hamstring/glute strength that protects your knees. Calf raises prevent shin splints. Clamshells and Copenhagen plank stabilize hips (IT band prevention). Dead bugs protect your lower back.

Progression: When an exercise feels easy (you finish all sets with reps to spare), increase weight by 5 lbs or move to a harder variation. Push-ups: use a lower stair. Pull-ups: slower negatives, then attempt a full rep.

The math: You burn ~2,800-3,000 cal/day with your activity. A 500-750 cal daily deficit = 1-1.5 lbs/week. That's 25-30 lbs by race day (225 → ~195-200).

Biggest lever: Nighttime snacking. Popcorn (air-popped, ~150 cal) is your best option. Ice cream → smaller bowl or 2x/week. Nachos → once/week max.

Rule: One snack per night. Serve a portion. Put the container away.

Meals: Don't overhaul dinner. Slightly smaller portions. Front-load calories earlier in the day. Skip liquid calories (water, black coffee, tea only).

Don't diet hard on training days. Eat normally on long run days and ultimate frisbee nights. You need the fuel.

Why weight matters for the race: Every pound lost is less impact on your joints per stride. At 13,000+ strides in a half marathon, even 10 lbs less makes a real difference in how your knees feel at mile 10.

Likely cause: Tight upper trapezius and levator scapulae on the left side, from desk posture (forward head position).

The fix: Chin tucks strengthen the deep neck flexors that hold your head in proper alignment. Levator stretch releases the tight muscle. Rotations restore range of motion.

Band pull-aparts (in upper body day) also help by strengthening the mid/lower traps that counteract forward-head posture.

Work posture: Monitor at eye level. Head stacked over shoulders, not craning forward. If you catch yourself leaning in, do a quick chin tuck.
